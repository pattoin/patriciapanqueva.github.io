Overview

Your Information Is Everywhere. Finding It Shouldn't Be Hard.

By 2019, Alexa users had accumulated personal data across a growing number of domains — calendar events, reminders, lists, email, contacts, shopping orders. Each domain had its own skill, its own utterance pattern, its own mental model. To find something, you had to already know where it lived.

Research surfaced a clear frustration: users couldn't reliably distinguish between a calendar event and a reminder. They knew they had asked Alexa to remember something — they just couldn't remember what they called it. And Alexa had no way to help them find it across domains.

Alexa Personal Data Search was the answer: a unified, domain-agnostic search experience that let users find any personal information — regardless of where it was stored — with a single natural language query.

The Problem

Three Hard Problems With No Obvious Answers

Core Challenge

Every piece of personal information in Alexa lived in a silo. Calendar, reminders, lists, email, shopping — each had its own data model, its own voice skill, its own interaction pattern. A user asking "Alexa, find the thing about my dentist" could get zero results if they'd stored it as a reminder rather than a calendar event.

There was no concept of unified search in the Alexa ecosystem. No cross-domain query. No fallback when a result wasn't found in the expected domain. Users blamed themselves when the real problem was the architecture.

Problem 01
Each domain was a silo
Calendar, reminders, lists, email, contacts — each lived in its own skill with no shared search layer. Finding information required knowing exactly which domain it was in.
Problem 02
The ordinals question
When multiple results existed, should Alexa say "your first result is..." or just read them straight? Ordinals add structure but also cognitive load. We had no data — we had to test.
Problem 03
No voice search precedent
Visual search has conventions — ranked lists, highlighted matches, filters. Voice has none. We were designing the interaction grammar for personal data search from scratch.
Problem 04
Cross-surface consistency
The same search had to work on a voice-only Echo Dot, an Echo Show with a full screen, and an Echo Spot with a tiny circular display. Three very different canvases for the same result.

"Users knew they had asked Alexa to remember something. They just couldn't remember what they called it — and Alexa had no way to help them find it."

Design Process

Building a Unified Search Experience

My approach was to design the interaction grammar first — the rules that would govern how results were spoken, how multiple results were navigated, and how zero results were handled — before touching visual design. The voice model had to be right before the screen layer could be built on top of it.

1
Map all personal data domains
Created a comprehensive inventory of every data type a user might want to find — calendar events, reminders, lists, email threads, contacts, shopping orders, notes. Mapped the mental models users had for each, and where those models overlapped or conflicted.
2
Define the unified query model
Designed a cross-domain intent that could route a natural language query to multiple domains simultaneously and surface results in a coherent, ranked order — regardless of which domain contained the answer.
3
Prototype the ordinals debate
Built and tested multiple response patterns — with ordinals ("Your first result is...") and without. Used Keynote conversation prototypes and Adobe XD multimodal prototypes to test with real users on UserTesting.com. The ordinals finding became one of the most debated design decisions of the project.
4
Design the one result vs. no result patterns
Designed distinct response patterns for zero results, one result, and multiple results — each requiring a different conversational shape, different visual treatment, and different recovery path for users.
5
Build the unified visual model
Designed a "Personal Information" unified view for Echo Show — a single card-based display that could render results from any domain in a consistent format, with the source domain labeled but de-emphasized.
6
Redline to the Alexa Design System
Produced complete UX specs and redlines aligned to the Alexa Elements design system — ensuring engineering could implement quickly without ambiguity across all three surfaces.
The Ordinals Debate

To Number or Not to Number

One of the sharpest design tensions in the project: when Alexa returns multiple results, should she number them? Ordinals add navigational structure but also cognitive overhead — saying "your third result" forces users to count mentally. Without ordinals, the list flows more naturally but users lose their place.

We tested both patterns with real users. The finding: ordinals helped significantly when there were 3+ results, but felt unnecessary and even slightly condescending with only 1–2. The shipped design used ordinals conditionally — only surfacing them when they added genuine navigational value.

With ordinals — shipped ✓
A: "You have 3 results for dentist. Your first result is a calendar event: Dentist appointment, Tuesday at 2pm. Your second result is a reminder: Call dentist about X-ray. Do you want to hear the third?"
Users could navigate back — "Go to the second one" — without losing context. Especially helpful on voice-only devices with no visual anchor.
Without ordinals — tested, not shipped
A: "You have 3 results for dentist. A calendar event: Dentist appointment, Tuesday at 2pm. A reminder: Call dentist about X-ray. A list item: Dentist insurance card."
Felt more natural for 1–2 results but broke down with 3+. Users couldn't refer back to specific items without re-listening to the whole list.
Research Insights

What Users Taught Us

01
Domain blindness is real. Users had no reliable mental model of the difference between a reminder and a calendar event. They just knew they'd "told Alexa something."
02
Zero results was the biggest failure mode. When Alexa found nothing, users assumed they had done something wrong — not that Alexa had looked in the wrong place.
03
Voice and touch needed to work together. On Echo Show, users naturally wanted to tap a result after hearing it. The voice interaction had to anticipate the shift to touch mid-session.
04
Ordinals aid navigation for 3+ results. Below that threshold, they felt formal and unnecessary. The final design used ordinals conditionally based on result count.
Voice Design

Response Patterns & Golden Utterances

Three distinct response patterns were designed — for one result, multiple results, and zero results. Each required its own conversational shape and recovery path.

Response Pattern — One result Voice Design Spec
C: "Alexa, find my dentist appointment"
A: "I found one result. A calendar event: Dentist, Tuesday March 12th at 2pm. Do you want me to add anything or is there anything else?"
Single result — no ordinal needed. Alexa states the domain (calendar event), the content, and offers a natural exit or follow-up action.
Response Pattern — Multiple results with ordinals Voice Design Spec
C: "Alexa, what do I have about the dentist?"
A: "I found 3 results. Your first result is a calendar event: Dentist, Tuesday at 2pm. Your second result is a reminder: Call dentist about X-ray. Do you want to hear the third result?"
C: "Yes"
A: "Your third result is a list item: Dentist insurance card, in your health list."
3+ results — ordinals enable navigation. Alexa pauses after 2 results and asks to continue, preventing information overload.
Response Pattern — No results Voice Design Spec
C: "Alexa, find my gym schedule"
A: "I couldn't find anything about gym schedule in your calendar, reminders, or lists. Would you like to add a reminder or create a new list?"
Zero results — Alexa names the domains she searched (shifting blame from user to system), then offers a constructive next step rather than a dead end.
Golden Utterances — what users actually say
"Alexa, find my dentist appointment" "Alexa, what do I have about the meeting?" "Alexa, search for mom's birthday" "Alexa, find the thing about my flight" "Alexa, what did I add about groceries?" "Alexa, look up my car service" "Alexa, find anything about the trip" "Alexa, what do I have this weekend?"
Outcome & Impact

What Shipped — and What It Changed

Impact

Alexa Personal Data Search shipped in 2019, giving users the ability to find any personal information across domains with a single natural language query — without needing to know which domain it lived in.

The project established the cross-domain search interaction grammar for Alexa — response patterns, ordinal conventions, zero-result recovery paths — that subsequent personal information features were built on.

Most meaningfully, it shifted the mental model: Alexa was no longer a collection of separate skills. She was a unified assistant who knew you — and could help you find anything you'd ever asked her to remember.

The ordinals research became an internally referenced study within the HHO team — one of the few cases where a single interaction decision was tested, documented, and shared across multiple product teams as a design standard.

Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

I would push for longitudinal research on search behavior — not just what users searched for in testing sessions, but what they failed to find over weeks of real use. The zero-result pattern we designed was good, but the data to make it great would have come from real failure cases at scale.

I'd also advocate earlier for a proactive suggestion layer — rather than waiting for users to search, Alexa could surface relevant personal information before being asked. That vision was in the Connect north star document; Personal Data Search was the foundation it needed to exist.
